How an air accident is investigated

Modern investigations follow ICAO Annex 13, the international standard. The sole objective is the prevention of future accidents — not to apportion blame or liability. The flow below shows how raw wreckage becomes a final report and, ultimately, safer skies.

Hours · State of Occurrence notified

Notification & team assembly

The State where the accident happened opens the investigation and appoints an Investigator-in-Charge. Other States (of the operator, manufacturer, engine maker, and States that lost citizens) send accredited representatives and advisers.

Days · Field phase

Site & wreckage examination

The crash site is documented and mapped; the wreckage is recovered, sometimes reassembled. Impact marks, fracture surfaces, fire patterns and component positions are read like a crime scene to reconstruct the final moments.

Days–weeks · The "black boxes"

Recorder recovery & readout

The Cockpit Voice Recorder (CVR) and Flight Data Recorder (FDR) are located by their underwater locator beacons, downloaded in a lab, and time-synchronised. Hundreds of parameters and cockpit audio rebuild exactly what the aircraft and crew did.

Weeks–months · Factual phase

Gathering the factual record

Maintenance logs, ATC tapes, radar, weather (METAR/TAF), crew rosters and medical history, witness statements, manufacturer data and metallurgy/component testing are collected into the factual report.

Months · Analysis

Analysis & sequence of events

Investigators build the chain of events and test hypotheses against the evidence, separating the probable cause from the contributing factors — the human, technical, organisational and environmental holes that lined up.

Draft → consultation

Findings & probable cause

Conclusions are drafted and circulated to involved States and parties for comment. Findings state what happened and why, in evidence-based, non-punitive language.

Outcome

Safety recommendations

The most important product: actionable recommendations to regulators and industry — design changes, Airworthiness Directives (ADs), new procedures, training and rule-making to close the gaps that allowed the accident.

Public · Often 1–3 years later

Final report & follow-up

The final report is published. Implementation of recommendations is tracked over years. Many of the safety features you fly with today exist because of a specific accident in this registry.

The four factor families

This registry tags every contributing factor into one of four families — a simple version of the models (SHELL, HFACS) investigators use to keep human and system causes in view.

Human factorsCrew actions, decisions, workload, fatigue, training, communication, situational awareness.
Technical / mechanicalStructure, systems, engines, sensors, software, design and manufacturing defects.
Organisational / systemicAirline culture, maintenance, oversight, certification, procedures, regulation.
Environmental / weatherWeather, icing, birds, terrain, runway condition, external hazards.

Why accidents are rarely one mistake

James Reason's "Swiss-cheese" model: every layer of defence has holes. An accident happens only when the holes momentarily line up. Investigators look for all the layers, not a single scapegoat.

Regulation
Organisation
Supervision
Preconditions

When a hole opens in every layer at once (≈ centre), the hazard becomes an accident.
